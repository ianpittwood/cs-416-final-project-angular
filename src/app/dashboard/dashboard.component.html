Income inequality has been a growing source of debate in recent years. While country's GDPs have been steadily raising over time, that new wealth hasn't made it to everyone. Over time, more wealth has continued to accumulate for the highest earners of society while the majority of the population has seen their wages stagnate. For some, the growth in wages has even been eclipsed by inflation. It's important for us to track and attempt to reduce inequality to avoid its worst effects on society, such as health problems, homelessness, and crime.

This project uses a multitude of metrics available from the World Bank and World Inequality Database to visualize the growing problem of inequality in the globe's richest nations, the G20. The core metric for measuring inequality is the Gini coefficient, which compares the cumulative proportions of the population against the cumulative proportions of the income they receive. It ranges from 0, indicating perfect equality, to 1, indicating perfect inequality. Theoretically, it can reach values beyond 1 in the case of massive debt or negative income in a portion of society.

A variety of other metrics are also provided. GDP and GDP per capita help convey the overall wealth and growth in wealth for a country. Income share breakdowns are also provides for all data points, showing the percentage of income going to each portion of society: the Top 1% of earners, the Next 9%, the Middle 40%, and the Bottom 50% of earners.
